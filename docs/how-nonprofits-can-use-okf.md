# How nonprofits can use the Open Knowledge Format

*A short, practical companion to the sample bundle. Written in a personal capacity; built at TechSoup. Drafted with AI assistance (Anthropic's Claude, Opus 4.8).*

## The problem

Civil society's knowledge is fragmented. Every food bank, every 211, every funder, every capacity-builder maintains its own list of resources, services, eligibility rules, and hard-won advice — in incompatible spreadsheets, CRMs, PDFs, and wikis. As nonprofits adopt AI, those silos become a hard ceiling: an agent is only as good as the context it can reach, and most of that context isn't machine-readable or shareable.

## The shift

The [Open Knowledge Format](https://github.com/GoogleCloudPlatform/knowledge-catalog/tree/main/okf) is a simple idea with large consequences: represent knowledge as a folder of markdown files with a little structured metadata. That's it. It's readable by a person in any editor, renderable on GitHub, and parseable by any AI agent — with no database, no vendor, and no lock-in. Because it's just files in version control, it can be *shared and federated* across organizations without anyone owning the pipe.

## What it unlocks for the sector

- **Resource directories (211 and beyond).** A community resource directory becomes a portable, agent-ready bundle. It complements existing standards like [Open Referral / HSDS](https://openreferral.org) by adding the verbose, contextual knowledge agents need to actually *help* someone.
- **Offer and program catalogs.** What's available, who qualifies, what to watch out for — kept as files, powering many tools (a website, a recommender, a chatbot) from one source.
- **Shared sense-making across organizations.** When many groups publish in the same format, the sector can see patterns it can't see today — what's available where, what's changing, where the gaps are.

## The civil-society opportunity

If even a fraction of the sector adopted a common, open knowledge layer, we could coordinate at a scale that has always been out of reach for under-resourced organizations — not by centralizing power in one platform, but by agreeing on a format and keeping our own data. That is infrastructure that builds local capacity instead of extracting from it.

This sample bundle is one concrete demonstration. The [civic profile](civic-profile.md) proposes the shared conventions the sector would need. The invitation is open.
